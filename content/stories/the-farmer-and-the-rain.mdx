The farmer repaired the fence before the first cloud appeared.

He sharpened the tools, cleared the stones, turned the soil, and walked the field each evening with the patience of someone who knew the land had its own calendar.

His neighbor watched from the road and laughed.

"You cannot command the rain," he said.

The farmer leaned on the fence post he had just set into the ground.

"No," he said. "But I can be ready when it comes."

## Prepared, not possessed

Control is often smaller than we want.

Influence is often larger than we notice.

The farmer could not move the sky. He could not negotiate with clouds or argue with the season. But he could repair the fence. He could prepare the soil. He could remove what would waste the rain when it finally arrived.

That distinction is easy to understand and hard to live.

We spend enormous energy trying to control outcomes that do not fully belong to us: how people respond, when opportunity arrives, whether timing is kind, whether luck cooperates, whether the world recognizes our effort on our preferred schedule.

Meanwhile, the work that does belong to us waits quietly.

The conversation we can have.

The skill we can sharpen.

The apology we can offer.

The system we can improve.

The field we can prepare.

> Wisdom is knowing which work belongs to you.

## The patience that is not passive

Patience is often misunderstood as waiting.

But the farmer was not waiting in the lazy sense. He was preparing without pretending that preparation guaranteed the outcome.

That is a more mature kind of patience. It does not confuse readiness with control. It does not demand that life obey effort immediately. It simply refuses to let uncertainty become an excuse for neglect.

There is dignity in that kind of work.

You do what can be done. You release what cannot be forced. You keep your hands honest.

## When the rain arrives

The rain came late.

It came after the neighbor had stopped joking and after the farmer had started to wonder whether this season would be harder than the last.

But when it came, the field received it.

The fence held. The soil opened. The water did not run uselessly across neglected ground.

The rain did not reward control.

It rewarded readiness.

This is one of the quiet laws of a good life: when you cannot control the arrival, prepare the receiving.
