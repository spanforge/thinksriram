## The verse

::verse
Sura Mandira Taru Muula Nivaasah,
Shayyaa Bhootalamajina Vaasah
Sarva Parigraha Bhoga Tyaagah,
Kasya Sukham Na Karoti Viraagah
::

## Transliteration

Sura-mandira taru-muula nivaasah / Shayyaa bhootalamajina vaasah / Sarva parigraha bhoga tyaagah / Kasya sukham na karoti viraagah

## Translation

Living under temple eaves or at the roots of trees, sleeping on the ground, having renounced all possession and pleasure — does not such dispassion bring joy to all?

## The teaching

A question about radical simplicity. The sage who has given up everything — comfort, possession, social status — and lives in the most basic conditions: is he not at peace? Shankara points to the paradox that the grasping after comfort often produces more discomfort than the embrace of simplicity.

## Living this today

The pull toward simpler living — fewer possessions, quieter days, deliberate withdrawal from noise — is a modern approximation of what this verse describes, and every generation rediscovers it in its own language. People who have reached peak consumption are discovering what Shankara knew: above a threshold, more stuff creates more noise, not more peace. This is not a call to live under a tree. But it is a call to examine how much of your life energy is spent maintaining possessions, obligations, and appearances that do not serve your wellbeing. Simplify deliberately. Subtract what is not adding. The spaciousness that results is not poverty — it is clarity. What you let go of makes room for what was already enough.
