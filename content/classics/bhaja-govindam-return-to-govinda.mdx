## The verse

::verse
Bhaja Govindam Bhaja Govindam,
Govindam Bhaja Moodhamate
Janma Janmaantara Sanchitapaapam,
Smarana Ekena Nashhyati
::

## Transliteration

Bhaja govindam bhaja govindam / Govindam bhaja moodhamate / Janma janmaantara sanchita-paapam / Smarana ekena nashyati

## Translation

Seek Govinda, seek Govinda, seek Govinda, O fool! The accumulated sins of many births are destroyed by a single act of sincere remembrance.

## The teaching

The final verse closes where the first opened — with the refrain. Its ending carries extraordinary grace: even lifetimes of accumulated wrong action, confusion, and harm can be dissolved by a single sincere act of turning toward what is real. This is not a promise of magical escape from consequences — it is an affirmation that the door of transformation is always open, always now, regardless of how far one has strayed.

## Living this today

No matter how long you have been distracted, how many years you have spent running from what matters, how many choices you regret — the return is always available. Now. This moment. There is no accumulated deficit of wrong living that bars you from beginning again. The first sincere breath taken in awareness is your Govinda. The first moment of genuine presence after years of absence is your beginning. Shankara ends his great song not with a threat but with an open door. Step through it. It is never too late to begin again, because beginning again is the whole practice.

Closing Reflection

Bhaja Govindam ends where it begins. The refrain does not resolve — it returns. This is intentional. Liberation, in Shankara's vision, is not a destination reached once and left behind. It is a practice of return: again and again, to the ground of your own being, to what is real beneath the noise of wanting and fearing.

The 31 verses you have just passed through are not 31 different teachings. They are 31 angles on a single teaching: wake up. See clearly. Stop mistaking the map for the territory. And seek — with whatever you have, from wherever you are — the Govinda that is the ground of your own existence.

This is not spiritual advice. It is practical wisdom, as true now as it was twelve centuries ago. The disruptions of any era — economic, political, personal, mortal — will separate those who know who they are from those who only know what they do. The first group will navigate; the second will be navigated.

Shankara's fool is not unintelligent. He is someone who has confused accumulation for arrival, performance for purpose, information for understanding. We are all, at times, that fool.

The invitation is always open. Begin again.

Bhaja Govindam.  Bhaja Govindam.  Govindam Bhaja.
