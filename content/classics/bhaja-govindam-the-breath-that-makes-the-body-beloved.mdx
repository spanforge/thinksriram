## The verse

Bhaaryaa Bibhyati Tasmin Kaaye
::

## Transliteration

Yaavat pavano nivasati dehe / Taavat prichchati kushalam gehe / Gatavati vaayau dehaapaaye / Bhaaryaa bibhyati tasmin kaaye

## Translation

As long as there is breath in the body, people enquire about your wellbeing. The moment the breath departs, even your spouse is afraid of the same body.

## The teaching

The most intimate bond — marriage — dissolves the moment life leaves the body. The person who shared your bed for decades becomes something to fear in death. Shankara uses this stark image not to terrify, but to illuminate: what is real cannot be the physical form. Something beyond the body must be cultivated if we are to live — and die — with peace.

## Living this today

Modern life excels at helping us avoid this truth. We medicalise death, hide it in institutions, scroll past it. But death-awareness is not morbid — it is clarifying. The Japanese practice of memento mori, the Stoic daily meditation on mortality — these are not pessimistic habits. They are the habits of people who take life seriously because they know it ends. Ask yourself today: if I had one year left, what would I stop doing? What would I start? Then start now. Live as though the ending is real, because it is.
