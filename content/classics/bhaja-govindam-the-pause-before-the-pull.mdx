## The verse

::verse
Dvaadashmanjarikaa Bhikshaashee,
Vishnorvishaye Dridhachittee
Sankalpavikalpana Rahitaha,
Naama Prabhaavanaamanasijee
::

## Transliteration

Dvaadasha-manjarikaa bhikshaashee / Vishnorvishaye dridha-chittee / Sankalpa-vikalpana rahitaha / Naama prabhaavanaa manasijee

## Translation

One who lives on alms, who is firm in devotion to Vishnu, who is free from desires and doubts — such a one has conquered the mind born of delusion.

## The teaching

This verse, attributed to Shankara's disciple Hastamalaka, describes the liberated sage: one who has renounced the game of accumulation, who holds a stable inner orientation, and who lives free from the oscillation of wanting and not-wanting. Freedom from sankalpa (intention-as-craving) and vikalpa (doubt) is the mark of a settled mind.

## Living this today

You do not need to become a monk. But the inner quality described here is available to all: a mind that is not constantly swinging between craving and aversion, hope and fear, attraction and repulsion. This is what meditation teachers call equanimity; what psychologists call affect regulation; what Vedantins call freedom from sankalpa-vikalpa. The practical version: notice your mind's oscillations today. When you feel pulled toward something or pushed away from something, pause. Is this genuine discernment, or just the habitual swing of the pendulum? Cultivate the pause. The pause is the beginning of freedom. Freedom is not the absence of the pull. It is the pause before you follow it.
